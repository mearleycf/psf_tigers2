Tigers

Tigers are a keystone species.

They're integral to the health of the ecosystems in which they live. As apex predators, they keep prey species under control. This protects the vegetation which in turn maintains the integrity of streams, forests and croplands that provide people around the world with clean air, water, food and financial benefits. When we protect tigers, we protect ourselves

Tiger stripes are like fingerprints.

No two are the same. The stripes are not only in the tiger’s fur, but are a pigmentation of the skin.

Tigers have a white spot on the back of both ears, which looks like eyes.

This tricks predators into thinking the tiger is looking at them. This is why they are called ‘eye spots’

Classroom Activities

Attention Students! Tell your Teacher!

Tired of sitting down in your seats all class? Try suggesting one of these activities to your teacher.

  1. Scavenger Hunt!

    Divide the class into teams of 4.Each team is responsible to use the website to develop 5 questions for a scavenger hunt using the trivia facts. The teams then exchange the questions with each other and see who can complete the scavenger hunt the fastest.

  2. First/Last/Worst/Best.

    Each student needs to pick one of the categories and write a tiger story. What was your first encounter with a tiger? When did you last think about a tiger? What is your worst experience with a tiger? What is the best experience you had with a tiger? Bonus points if you can incorporate a fact from the website.

  3. Our Favorite Fact.

    Have teams of 4 pick their two favorite tiger facts. Have a student facilitate the class discussion and write all of the selected facts on the board (if a fact is chosen more than once, use tallies to represent the votes). Label all of the facts with a different letter. Use the student facilitator to find a way to get the class to agree on their favorite fact. Then, have the class visualize the fact and display it in the room (mural, pictures, photo gallery, etc.)

Who Am I?

The tiger (Panthera tigris) is recognizable for its pattern of dark vertical stripes on reddish-orange fur with a lighter underside. The species is classified in the genus Panthera with the lion, jaguar, leopard, and snow leopard. It is territorial and generally a solitary but social predator, often requiring large contiguous areas of habitat that support its prey requirements.

Tigers once ranged widely across eastern Eurasia, from Eastern Anatolia Region in the west to the Amur River basin, and in the south from the foothills of the Himalayas to Baliin the Sunda islands. Since the early 20th century, tiger populations have lost at least 93% of their historic range and have been extirpated in Western and Central Asia, in the islands of Java and Bali, and in large areas of Southeast and South Asia and China. Today, they range from Siberian temperate forests to subtropical and tropical forests on the Indian subcontinent. The tiger has been classified as Endangered by the International Union for Conservation of Nature since 1986. As of 2015, the global population in the wild was estimated to number between 3,062 and 3,948 mature individuals, down from around 100,000 at the start of the 20th century, with most remaining populations occurring in small pockets isolated from each other. Major reasons for population decline include habitat destruction, habitat fragmentation and poaching.
